Plan Cddr: Backup, Primer and Restore

The inspiration for the Big Vision for what became Chenla happened in 1997, when I was living in Osaka. I was sitting at my desk on the third floor office where I worked. The room was damp and mildewy and infused with the smell of the thousands of cigarettes that the girls in the office chain smoked throughout the day, when I came across an article on the Web about the 10,000 Year clock. The clock was to be a mechanical computer, two floors tall, that would be housed in a cave, and run with little or no human intervention for 10,000 years.1

But what struck me back in '97 was the brief mention that a library was planned to go along with the clock. I was hooked, and I knew that I wanted to build that library.

As I began working through the challenges of building such a library, It quickly became apparent that the library with the clock would be little more than a time-capsule intended for the distant future. And while that was an interesting problem in itself, the real challenge would to create a living library that would grow over time and could survive 10,000 years, and the collapse of civilization, not once, but many times.

To do that, the library would have to be distributed, with many copies scattered around the planet, or even on the moon or Mars if people like Elon Musk are able to fulfill their goal of making mankind a multi-planet species.

Collapse is inevitable. Over millions of years, earth's climate oscillates between a carbon rich green house and a oxygen rich cold house dominated by an ice age. The collapse of ecosystems, followed by mass extinctions are a regular cycle in the earth's long history.

Human civilizations have followed a similar pattern of growth and collapse in the brief 10,000 years since mankind first developed civilization. Some 40 civilizations, depending on your definition of civilization, have risen and fallen already. The next collapse is not a matter of if, but when, and then how total the collapse will be, and the subsequent rebuilding that comes afterwards.

1999 Taiwan Earthquake

This is especially relevant today because, for the first time in human history, we now effectively have a single global interdependent civilization. The economic shock wave from the earthquake that rocked Taiwan in September 1999 was felt around the world for years.

A significant proportion of the world supply of RAM was at the time made in Taiwan, and the six-day shutdown of Hsinchu Science Park and other factories resulting from the quake caused computer memory prices to triple on world markets.

– Wikipedia, 921 Earthquake

2011 Bangkok Floods

More recently, the 2011 Bangkok floods had a global impact:

The economies of other countries besides Thailand will be significantly impacted by the flood. The country that will be hardest hit is Japan. Japanese firms with plants in Thailand include Toyota, Honda, Hitachi, and Canon. One analyst predicted the profits of one firm, Toyota, may be cut by ¥200 billion (US$2.5 billion). Worker incomes in Thailand and Japan are also impacted.

– Wikipedia, 2011 Thailand Floods

The economic impact of the Bangkok floods on Japan ended up being greater than the Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami in 2011. The Bangkok floods, significantly impacted the world economy as a whole.

Thailand's flood helped contribute to a total estimated US$259 billion in economic losses for the first nine months of 2011. These losses represented 80% of the world's total economic losses and the insurance industry has responded by raising rates in some areas between 50 and 200 percent or by outright not accepting new clients in Asia.

– Wikipedia, 2011 Thailand Floods

The global impact of these natural disasters stems from the way the global civilization is structured. Our civilization is not evenly distributed. There are many examples of places in the world that have a virtual a monopoly on the knowledge and means of production in producing a narrow set of things that are vital to the global civilization and economy.

If you knock one of these areas out, the entire world will be effected for years, but things will recover. The real danger is if several of these places are knocked out by any combination of natural disaster, war, or disease at the same time. The impact of such a scenario could well be enough to trigger a decline and eventual collapse of global civilization that can not be recovered from.

Khmer Rouge, Cambodia

I live in Cambodia and collapse here is no abstract concept. Cambodia is even now still in the process of a complete restore of their culture and civilization after the Khmer Rouge killed over 2 million people.

Pol Pot was what Charles Stross called in his novel Scratch Monkey, a Year Zero Man, who believed the only way to realize a communist utopia was to basically kill everyone from the old society and start over again from year zero. The Khmer Rouge didn't only target people, but history, culture, music, and education as well. More was lost than will ever be known.

When I was living here twelve years ago, there was almost no infrastructure, the roads in the capital city Phnom Penh were little more than ruts. You could still hear gun fire at night, and many buildings like the Red Cross building were little more than burnt out shells. People were still in sort of a state of shock, and were guarded. Flash forward till today and you would never recognize the city. A new 38 floor sky scrapper which is nearly finished can be seen from my balcony, and poking through the trees the pinnacle of the beautifully restored 13th century pagodga, Wat Phnom can be seen. Across a broad green belt the future Securities and Exchange Building which will house the new Cambodia Stock Market is slowly emerging from the ruins of an older government building. But there are still many gaps that haven't been filled. Even restoring Cambodian music and cuisine is an ongoing work in progress. And they're having to make it all up as they go along. It's a long painful process that is made far more difficult because so much of what was destroyed was lost forever.

The 10,000 Library, Revisted

MAKE SOME GODDAMN COPIES PEOPLE. CIVILISATION DOESN'T BACKUP ITSELF.

– Danny O'B ‏@mala \\Twitter 2013-01-15

Going back to the original concept of a 10,000 year library, the question becomes what would it be for?

For a library that is designed to be used for thousands of years, it's purpose must also address the most critical problems that mankind will face over the next 10,000 years. And clearly, that problem will be the cyclical collapse of complex human societies and the survival of the human species over time spans of hundreds of thousands of years or more into the future.

As we have already discussed, collapse is not evenly distributed. Smaller collapses are more common than total collapse. And it is hoped that in improving the way that we deal with local collapse, and ensure that what is critical to societies and cultures are preserved, so that they can be restored, that we can forestall, the instances of total collapse whenever possible. And even when there is a total collapse, that enough is preserved and remembered to shorten the time it will take to restore civilization and mitigate some of the long term consequences that would otherwise sink humanity into another Dark Age that could last centuries.

When civilizations truly crash, no one at the time can imagine the depth of the fall, nor how labored and long the revival, if it ever happens. After Rome fell, “Europe went unwashed for a thousand years.” Cities emptied; literacy vanished. All the Roman achievements of engineering, culture, and government simply ceased to be. Population in some areas dropped by nine-tenths. Even with the heroic continuity of the Catholic Church, the skein of culture was reduced to fragile wisps: Only one copy of Lucretius made it through the Dark Ages, only one copy of five books of Livy, one copy of nine plays of Euripides, one copy of Tacitus, one copy of Beowulf.

– Steward Brand, The Clock of the Long Now

So how do we go about building a library that can help buffer the impact of different collapses over thousands of years? Computers face their own version of collapse on a regular basis. Equipment failure, data corruption, accidental erasure and malicious destruction are all too common with computer systems. To mitigate such disasters, you make a backup of the system, which is a snapshot of a running system at the time the backup was made.

In the event of a system failure, restoring the system is as simple as wiping the memory of the system and copying the backup and then restarting the system. If all goes well, you will only have lost changes since the last backup was made.

This is a good way of thinking about the library. The library should a Backup of civilization, and compiled into a Primer that can be used to Restore civilization in a structured and systematic manner.

Primer

The idea of a Primer to restore civilization is not new, though I haven't researched this yet. I was first introduced to the idea as a teenager in the novel, The Mote in God's Eye by Larry Niven and Jerry Pournelle, where an alien race maintains a museum and library of weapons technology that is needed when the aliens periodically wipe each other out.

But perhaps a little closer to what we are looking for was proposed by the biologist James Lovelock.

James Lovelock…. proposed compiling a start-up manual for civilization, beginning with how to make fire, moving on through all of science and technology, from subjects such as ancient genetic design (domesticating plants and animals by selective breeding) to current genetic design (cloning).

– Steward Brand, The Clock of the Long Now

To call it a primer is a a bit of a misnomer, in that the primer would dwarf even the very largest of today's libraries. But it would be a primer in that it would first provide an introduction to any number of concepts and topics to an unknown audience, sometime in the future, who may have even lost written writing. No assumptions can be made except that optimistic assumption there will be an audience left alive to read the primer.

The primer would contain three core categories of information. The first is descriptive information, which includes the vast majority of cultural, scientific and academic recorded works, including books, periodicals, audio recordings, images, video etc.. The second is proscriptive information, which includes recipes, instructions, methods, methods and manuals for building and doing things. Finally, there would be data which includes any type of unstructured recorded tabular or imaging data. This would include anything from population records, to weather data, to satellite telemetry and imaging.

Backup

The Backup is, more than anything else a process, community and a kind of hybrid institution. It's core mission is in two parts. First the Backup will be responsible for gathering the materials that will become part of the primer, organize and keep them up to date and safe. Second, the backup will ensure that organizations who do provide libraries which are open to the public are able to maintain complete collections so that works aren't lost to general circulation. In other words they will will provide public libraries with a source of last resort for any work in the Arc's collection.

At the heart of the backup will be a distributed network of dark archives. We call them dark because they will not be connected directly to the Internet, though the people maintaining the Backup will be. The Dark Arcs as we're calling them, will not be accessible by the general public either.

In fact, the less people know about them the better. Mankind has a long history of devolving into long bouts of temporary insanity. Censorship and book burning and general destruction of recorded information is an age old pass time, which will not change in the future.

Keeping mankind's collective memories and experience safe will require that there are many copies in as many locations as possible, and ensure that during periods of temporary insanity, as we are seeing today in fundamentalist religious groups from all religions, as well by authoritarian governments who want to rewrite history for their own purposes.

For the backup to succeed, it will have to be designed so that no matter how much damage people do, that some copies will survive the purges to be restored when sanity has been restored.

Restore

Rebuilding from a complete systemic collapse is not as simple as handing people an iPad with a copy of the primer. As Charles Stross wrote, "it takes an industrial civilization, to build an industrial civilization." A complete restore will take generations, even if everything goes well, and with access to comprehensive primer. The alternative is to reinvent everything from scratch, not knowing the pitfalls or the optimal way to accomplish what needs to be done. This could well take centuries or even millennia.

Like the Backup, the Restore is a process that can be adapted to meet the needs of what needs to be restored and within the constraints of what is available.

The Restore would, as Locklock said, start with the basics of survival, meeting the base levels of Maslows Hierarchy of Needs, food, shelter, and physical safety, and then progress up the ladder, by adding increasing complexity, structure and technology layer by layer.

Over long periods of thousands of years, the backup process, and primer will thoroughly field tested and used to restore different parts of civilization, and perhaps even the entire global civilization if things get bad enough. After each restore the experience of backing up and restoring will be refined, to limit the scope of whatever had caused the collapse, mitigate its worst effects and dramatically shorten the time needed to reestablish civilization.

The Vision Vs. Implementation

There is no question that the vision outlined here is not a trivial endeavour to even contemplate, let alone to execute. But it's also true that it is valuable, indeed, even crucial to the future survival of our civilization and perhaps even our species.

So, I hear you saying, how to get started?

We have been working for nearly a decade on develop data structures and markup languages and a framework that is designed for preserving context as well as the individual items in the collection over very long time frames which we call the Burr Metadata Framework (BMF).

BMF will be used as the foundation for our long-term archive and library software framework which is called Saltmine that will power the archives and Studyhalls.

Second, we have been developing the Studyhall Template as a fundamental institutional building block for the project, which can be used for education, research, development, the establishment of archives, and connections to existing public-facing libraries. Since Studyhalls are federated and distributed, and designed to be setup anywhere, relatively inexpensively. Our hope is that the Template will be eventually adopted around the world.

Finally, we are in the process of turning this essay into a full blown detailed proposal. The proposal will be in the form of a book(s) that will set out our initial vision for the Studyhalls, Primer, Backup and Restore. We are calling this, Plan Cddr.2

Plan Cddr will complement other large scale global initiatives like Rocky Mountain Institute's Reinventing Fire, and Plan B from the Lester Brown's Earth Policy Institute which are taking on the daunting task of mobilizing people, companies and governments to tackle the challenge of addressing the combined challenges of climate change, population growth, water resource allocation and manage, food and energy production. We aim to the the library and knowledge base for all of these initiatives and countless others. But will also lay the groundwork for surviving and rebuilding in the event that they fail.

We are in the early stages of establishing the first Studyhall in Phnom Penh Cambodia and complete work on the first release of Saltmine. A second Studyhall will be established Vientiane, Laos PDR in late 2014.

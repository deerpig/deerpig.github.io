A Failure of Imagination

Posted on 10 Apr 2014 by Brad Collins

McGuire took Benjamin to one side and stared at him intently before saying "I just want to say one word to you. Just one word."

"Yes, sir," Benjamin said.

"Are you listening?" McGuire said.

"Yes, I am."

"Plastics."

"Exactly how do you mean?"

– The Graduate, 1967

The human mind is a pattern recognition machine. The brain relentlessly takes in sensory input, and matches that input against memories and then makes a mental model that fits the sensory input into that mental model. The result, which is the illusion our brain creates, is a best guess at what the world is.

So long as the brain can relate new input with existing memories, analogies and metaphors, it does a pretty good job of understanding the world around us. But it's far more conjecture than we realise.

For example, the actual area that the human eye can see clearly is actually very small. But as the eye scans a room, the brain builds up a composit image that we think is much larger. But in fact it is a mental model that is made up of more of short term memories than what it is really seeing at any given moment.

The brain is constantly doing this. When you walk into a room you have never been in before, your mind makes assumptions about that room that are not supported by any direct evidence. We assume, on past experience that when we see a wall, there will be either another room, or outside of the building there. If you are in a submarine the brain will assume that there is water on the otherside of a wall, or another compartment.

This is far from being a perfect system, and the mental models that the brain generate are often wrong. The brain is driven to constantly and relentlessly look for patterns that can be added into a mental model. And when there is insufficient information, the mental model is influenced by what you want to see. If you are in a lifeboat scanning the horizon or sky for rescue, a chance reflection, or shadow will often be briefly percieved as a plane or boat. People who have very deeply held ideological and religious belief systems will often take false possitive matches as evidence for building a mental model that believes that they have actually seen things that in fact were never there.

But this problem is not the sole domain of people who have seen Elvis or Jesus in a grapefruit. Humans have a very real limit on the amount of change that they can incorporate into their mental model of the world at any given moment. If that change requires a person to set aside set beliefs, the brain rebels and refuses to accept the evidence before it. People can only change so much in a lifetime after they have reached a certain age. And once you've hit that limit, you've lost the ability to adapt.

As Alvin and Heidi Toffler have argued since their ground breaking book Future Shock was published in 1970, the rate that things are changing is increasingly exponentially. But the human brain, which is designed for life as hunter-gather species living on the African Savannah, has to cope with a world that changes more in a year than it's ancient ancestors would have to cope with in a lifetime.

It takes us time to internalize new ideas and concepts. And if a new idea is replaced with another idea before a person has had time to internalize it, they will be left behind and stuck trying to use an outmoded model of the world around them to understand a world that is diverging further and further from their own internal mental model.

William Gibson said that "the future is here, it's just not evenly distributed." The Toffler's divide human development into three broad stages, the first was was agricultural, the second was industrial, and the third is based on information, and I would add, networking. The agricultural revolution is still alive and well in various remote locations on the planet, and certainly the industrial revolution is in progress in the developing world. The post-industrial information-networking revolution has really only taken hold in highly developed areas of the planet in the last twenty years. Within each of these revolutions are many sub-stages of development. The agracultural revolution lasted milennia. The industrial revolution took place over hundreds of years. But the same amount of change that was spread over the industrial revolution has been compacted down into less than a single lifetime, and we are still in the early stages of the process.

So what is the process that we first understand something that is new, and then internalize it into our mental model?

Understanding all about context. There can be communcation between two parties unless they share a certain amount of information. That includes not only language, but cultural, economic and technological assumptions about the world around them. If you don't understand the context that the person you are communicating with, there can be no understanding and no communication will take place.

So when a person is confronted with something they don't understand they first try to match what they see with something that they do understand, even if they are very different things.

A common way of doing this is through the use of metaphors.

Technology has largely only found mass acceptance when a suitable metaphor has been created that give people a cognitive foot hold they can use to understand it.

People used a graphical display that had a desktop where you could arrange different things on your computer in the same way you would arrange paper, books and tools on your desk. If you wanted to throw something away, you deleted it by moving it into a trash can. Windows were closer to pieces of paper or books that could be moved around and placed on top of each other.

The Microsoft Office productivitiy suite of applications was a perfect example of this. A word processor, was an electronic type writer. A spreadsheet was a ledger book. Outlook, used for email, was the postal system, with an inbox and outbox, and letters that were addressed to reciepients like memos in an office. The presentation software was foamcore on an easel – or slides projected from a slide projector. These were metaphors that the general public could understand.

When the Internet came along, it didn't take off until the World Wide Web made it possible to introduce a whole new set of metaphors. A web site was like a storefront, or book, or magazine or catalog or newspaper. Yahoo didn't start as a search engine, but an online directory of web sites like the Yellow Pages.

Search never really had a proper metaphor linking it to the analog world, but it wasn't able to really take off until people had internalized the concepts behind software, operating systems and the web before they could internalize it.

The same happened with Social Media. The metaphors behind social media were the Bulletin Board, a part of a wall where one could pin notes, ads and pictures for other people to see. The other metaphor was the mailing list – which you subscribed to and sent email to and copies would then be sent to every one that had subscribed.

But there are only so many metaphors that people can successfully internalize. My father bought an early Macintosh that came with AOL. He learned enough to be able to read and send email, and to use instant messenger. But that was about it. As the Web and other services became more popular, he just couldn't make the cognitive leap that AOL was only a subset of the Internet. My father is far from being alone. Many thousands of people have never made it beyond AOL. It's almost as if AOL is Whoville, in Dr. Suess's Horton Hears a Who, but they believe that the tiny speck of dust they are living on is a planet and that there is nothing beyond it….

The problem is that most people who have been left behind, never understand that they are behind. They have a fuzzy idea that their mental model doesn't quite fit, but then they dismiss the idea because, it works just fine for them.

Anyone who has started a tech startup that working on the bleeding edge will be all too familiar with this problem.

When pitching ideas for new startups, everything is new and the founders fully understand that it will take some amount of effort to get people to understand what they are building and why it's a good idea. But time and time again, it seems that most potential investors simply are too far behind the technological curve for them to even grasp technoligies that are in common use, let alone new technolgies that will replace those.

Quite recently, I was thrown into a pitch without being told that I was giving a pitch until it was already happening. I explained that we are building a distributed cloud service for SE Asia that will adapt wildly successful cloud services worth hundreds of millions of dollars for developing markets in the region. But for some reason, they couldn't get their head around the concept of a Cloud service, because they couldn't make the distinction between an Telco and an ISP. We were conceptually lumped in with a Telco that he knew in Phnom Penh which had spent millions of dollars and failed. He had a rough concept of a Telco, which used networks and computers to provide communication services. And since many Telcos offer Internet connectivity, Internet services were just part of a Telco. So the idea of a Cloud Service was several generations of technology behind, it was impossible for him to grasp the concept, and we were dismissed out of hand.

In other cases, you might be able to explain a new technology in relation to a metaphor that they do understand. So you might make a pitch around the Search metaphor. Well, this company is a little like Google Search in that is does A, B and C. But it is not search because it does D, E, F. But you lost them as soon as you said it's like Google because in their mind you're building a search engine.

This is always compounded by potential investors who sort of understand what you are pitching, but then go to a trusted friend who is a self proclaimed expert who has no idea what they are talking about. The result is inevitably the same. "Sorry, we can't become involved", they say, "it's already been done." The fact that when Google and Facebook had started, both search and social sites had certainly "already been done" was irrelevant.

When …… was pitching Hotmail around Silicon Valley, he was nearly laughed out of town. People couldn't grasp the concept of webmail.

This is very much a failure of imagination, an inability not only to understand what something is, but also a failure to see the potential of something that they do understand in the future.

Even people who have been at the cutting edge of technological development get it wrong sometimes. Bill Gates refused to integrate Microsoft products with the Internet until it was almost too late. And Steve Ballmer waited so long to develop a proper mobile platform that is probably is too late.

I have a pretty good track record in recognizing technoligies that will become big in the future. But I've gotten it wrong too. When we were running our Hong Kong ISP, Huge Net in the mid-90's we had one customer who gave away free Web Page hosting. He kept adding new customers, added servers and kept upgrading his bandwidth to handle the traffic. And yet he had no revenue stream. He gave it all away for free, and was loosing a lot of money every month in the process.

This went on for nearly three years, when suddenly he told us that he had sold his company for $20 million. We didn't have deep enough pockets to have done this ourselves, but we'd completely missed the emerging business model at the time to build up value in a company by building up a large user base and then selling the company without ever trying to generate revenue from the customer base.

Is there any way of reaching these people who are trapped in hopelessly out of data metaphorical loops? In most cases, the answer is likely no. Because the reason the future is distributed, is not only not evenly distributed, it can never be evenly distributed as each of us hits that wall and are left behind to spend the rest of our lives trapped in techological conceptual tital pools.

Over very long periods of time, this context is all but lost, and the meaning of things will drastically change. Homer's Odyssey and Illiad were not written, they were stories that evolved as part of the Oral Tradition before writing systems had been invented in the Greek States. They were not so much an art form, as a means of preserving the complexities of Greek culture and passing them on from one generation to another. The Muses were not inspirational, they represented the collection of tools that the poet used to memorize and perform these works and to pass them on to future generations unchanged. They acted as a kind of encyclopedia for a culture and complex society.

But that original context surrounding Homer's works was nearly forgotten as man moved from an Oral Tradition to a Written Tradition. In the Written Traditional, narrative prose is a far better means of recording and passing on information to other people today and to future generations. So poetry and Homer's encyclopedia gradually became known only as a story, as entertainment and studied in a context where poetry is a creative work of art.

if you can see the relationship of something you don't know, to something you do understand

Whether we realize it or not, people who had internalized a number of different early stages of the info-network revolution have now hit their ability to significantly change their mental models and are being left behind.

What a Difference a Month Makes

Posted on 31 Mar 2014 by Brad Collins

Our Web server went down a month ago. Sorry about that. We're now back up, using Github Pages to host the site. Thanks Github!

Our new building is no longer the ghost (but not rat or coackroach) infested, decrepit shell that it was when we signed the lease. We've made an enormous amount of progress in the last six weeks as these photos will attest.

We have installed awnings on the side of the building as well as a fixed metal awning on the first floor and a rollout vinyl awning on the ground floor.

The first floor (American 2nd floor) is all but finished with the walls and ceiling patched up, cleaned and painted, and the floors sanded, the screws resunk, and stained and shellacked. We have also completely replaced the chaos of electricical wiring and the fuse box. We can now handle the load from all of the computers that we will be adding and nothing will short out.

We have also changed our original idea, and have swapped the office and conference/podcasting studio. Below is what will be the studio side.

And the side with the windows will become the office side. We can comfortably fit up to 12 desks into this space.

Heading downstairs, we have cut a new door that connects the downstairs to the staircase leading upstairs. Before, if you wanted to go upstairs you had to go out the front and down and alley to get to the entrance.

The toilet still needs some work, but looks much better already. The tile floor was replaced, the plumbing fixed and a new sink installed.

Behind the toilet is the server room. There isn't much to see, it's just a plain white room with a tile floor. We can fit up to four full height racks in here.

The awful cabinates and several walls were knocked out for the tutorial center, which will have enough round for five or six workstations which can comfortably seat both a tutor and a student together.

The last of the demolition was done here in the Naga Shop less than four days ago.

There was a large window between the Naga Shop and the Cafe dining area. This has been removed and replaced with a wooden counter. This is Ruben working on the wood for the counter.

The door has been removed going into the kitchen, the squat toilet turned into a pantry and a large window and pickup counter has replaced a lot of the wall. This is very much still a work in progress.

There is still a lot of work left to be done, but we've come a long long ways in the last six weeks.

Our New Building

Posted on 11 Feb 2014 by Brad Collins

We are very pleased to announce that we have finally managed to lease a building here in Phnom Penh. We had been promised a building last year by persons who shall not be named. But the national elections and the uncertainty after the elections were contested put many things on permanent hold. So we made the decision to go ahead and rent a building ourselves.

The new location is #155 E0/E1 on Street 110, in Daun Penh in Phnom Penh. The location is very good. The building can be clearly seen from Nordodom Blvd. and is only a street over from our present office that is located in Brad's flat on Street 108.

As you can see from the next few shots, the ground floor will need a bit of work.

The left side will be a cafe, with a large kitchen in the back – the wall with the door will be largely knocked out and replaced with a counter.

The right side will need the most work. The half floor will be knocked out entirely and turned into our Naga General Supply shop.

Behind the shop, this forlorn looking area will be enlarged and become the studyhall's tutorial and scanning center.

Don't worry, the toilet will be completely rebuilt.

Behind the tutorial room, not pictured, will be our server room which will be large enough to hold four 19" racks.

There is also a very nice patio at the back of the building on the first floor.

The first floor, which we will be using as office space is in far better shape than the ground floor.

We got the keys about five days ago and are starting the long process of rennovation. The building will be the headquarters for Chenla and our sister for-profit company, Repozit as well as the location for our first Studyhall.

We'll be blogging our progress as things move along.

Douglas C. Engelbart (1925-2013) R.I.P.

Posted on 04 Jul 2013 by Brad Collins

Doug Engelbart died on Tuesday at the age 88 from kidney failure at his home in Atherton, California. Engelbart is best known as the inventor of the mouse. Let's just get it out of the way for those who haven't seen it. Here is the YouTube video of Engelbart's famous Mother of all Demos:

But Engelbart's vision and contribution went far beyond the mouse. Dave Crocker said it best on the IETF mailing list:

Besides the considerable technical contributions of Doug's project at SRI, theirs was a group that did much to create the open and collaborative tone of the Internet that we've come to consider as automatic and natural, but were unusual in those days.

Engelbart's real goal was to augment human intelligence. Bret Victor has an excellent post that dispells many of the misconceptions of Engelbart's work.

You can find an archive of all of his papers at the Doug Engelbart Institute. There is a very good page at CIS 471 which has collected a bunch of images and links that you might be interested in.

Here's the obligatory link to his wikipedia article. And obituaries from NYT, Chicago Tribune, LA Times, Rueters and The Register.

Our very own BMF (Burr Metadata Framework) was largely inspired by several of Englart's papers. They are well worth the time spent reading them and we hope that his work will continue to be remembered and inspire others for many years to come. Thank you Doug Engelbart, we all owe you an enormous debt of gratitude.

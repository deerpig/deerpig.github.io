Inspiration

The Studyhall Project has been inspired by a number of different concepts and projects which share a belief in creating local community-based spaces for students have access to one on one tutoring and computing.

Chinese Studyhalls

A common feature found in old prosperous Chinese walled villages throughout China is a building which is often next to or attatched to the Ancestral Hall which is known simply as the Studyhall. Studyhalls are often adorned with brightly painted sculptures depicting auspicious symbols like peaches which are meant to help students succeed in their studies.

Studyhalls have been used for a variety of purposes at different times in history, from being a place for students to study and be privately tutored, to the Chinese equivilent of the venerable American one-room-schoolhouse.

In a culture where families are traditionally packed together into very tight living spaces, the studyhall is a place apart from the noise and chaos of family life where students can find the peace and quiet needed to concentrate on their studies.

The studyhall is also an intensely local space which is an extension of home, which reinforces that the success of student is important to the success of your family and clan.

In poverty stricken communities all along the Mekong, these are virtues and themes worth emulating and reinforcing.

826 National

Another important influence on the studyhall project is 826 National. According to Wikipedia:

826 National is a nonprofit organization dedicated to helping students, ages 6–18, with expository and creative writing at eight locations across USA. The chapters include 826 Valencia in San Francisco, 826NYC in Brooklyn, New York, 826LA in Los Angeles, CA, 826CHI in Chicago, 826 Seattle, 826michigan in Ann Arbor, 826 Boston in Boston, and 826DC in Washington, DC (formerly the Capitol Letters Writing Collective). The 826 National mission is based on the understanding that great leaps in learning can happen with one-on-one attention, and that strong writing skills are fundamental to future success. Each 826 chapter provides drop-in tutoring, class field trips, writing workshops, and in-schools programs—all free of charge. 826 chapters are especially committed to supporting teachers, publishing student work, and offering services for English language learners.

  • Champasak Studyhall Store

    We are still unsure if the retail storefront will make the cultural transition from the United States to the Mekong river basin, but we've come up with some interesting concept for a possible store along the same lines as 826 National.

    The Phaya Naga is a mythical river creature which is believed to inhabit the Mekong River. It is a bit like Loch Ness' Nessie in Scotland, or Lake Champlain's Champie in Vermont except the Naga are a species rather than a specific individual.

    On the last day of Buddhist Lent, thousands of people gather on the banks of the Mekong in Nonkai, Thailand to watch for the Naga to shoot fireballs into the sky. Nongkai hosts a water parade and festival to welcome the Naga each year.

    In 2003, an investigative television reporter brought a camera into the bushes on the Lao side of the river and captured footage of Lao soldiers shooting tracer rounds into the sky.

    The incident sparked a huge kerfuffle in Nonkai. Spirited town hall meetings were hastily organized by the Nonkai city government who were scared of loosing an important tourist attraction. Scientists were produced who claimed the fireballs were caused by vegetation which fermented on the bottom of the river which created gas clouds.

    So we thought it fitting that the storefront would be a Phaya Naga Supply Store. Possible products could include Naga food (which can also be used to feed Koi and other fish), Naga Eggs (small watermellons which are sold in a branded box in a small straw nest, Naga heartburn medicine (those fireballs are caused from indigestion) and even a field guide to spotting and identifying different types of Naga in the wild.

One Laptop Per Child

According to Wikipedia:

The One Laptop Per Child Association (OLPC) is a U.S. non-profit organization set up to oversee the creation of an affordable educational device for use in the developing world. Its mission is "to create educational opportunities for the world's poorest children by providing each child with a rugged, low-cost, low-power, connected laptop with content and software designed for collaborative, joyful, self-empowered learning." Negroponte states that the mission is to eliminate poverty. Its current focus is on the development, construction and deployment of the XO-1 laptop…. As of October 2009, there are 1.6 million free books available for OLPC computers.
